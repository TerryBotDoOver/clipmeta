In the world of stock footage, metadata is your silent salesperson. It’s what ensures your footage finds its way to the buyers who need it. Yet, all too often, metadata is either an afterthought or a rushed job. This can be a costly mistake. Metadata that’s inaccurate, incomplete, or poorly structured can bury your content in the depths of stock libraries like Adobe Stock, Shutterstock, and Pond5, far from the eyes of potential buyers.

Before you hit upload on your next batch, take a moment to run through this metadata review checklist. This isn't just another task on your to-do list; it's a critical step in ensuring your footage is discoverable and appealing to buyers.

## Titles: The First Impressions Matter

**Do: Use Descriptive, Specific Titles**

Imagine you're a buyer looking for footage of a bustling New York street. Which title would catch your eye: "Street Scene" or "Time-Lapse of Busy New York City Street with Yellow Taxis"? The latter not only tells you what the footage is about but also gives you a sense of the scene's vibrancy and location.

**Don't: Use Vague or Redundant Titles**

Avoid titles like "Video Clip 1" or "Nature Footage." These are too generic and don’t provide any valuable information to the buyer. Instead, aim for specificity: "Aerial View of Lush Green Forest Canopy in Spring."

## Descriptions: Context is Key

**Do: Provide Contextual Details**

Descriptions should expand on the title by offering additional context. For example, if your title is "Time-Lapse of Busy New York City Street with Yellow Taxis," a good description might be: "A dynamic time-lapse capturing the hustle and bustle of a New York City street during peak afternoon traffic, featuring classic yellow taxis and diverse pedestrians."

**Don't: Overload with Keywords**

While it might be tempting to stuff your description with keywords, this can actually deter buyers. A description like "Street, New York, City, Taxi, Traffic, Fast, Cars, People, Urban" reads awkwardly and doesn’t add any real value. Instead, focus on crafting a narrative that complements the visual content.

## Keywords: The Search Engine's Best Friend

**Do: Use Relevant and Varied Keywords**

Keywords are crucial for searchability. Use a mix of broad and specific terms. If your footage is of a beach sunset, include keywords like "beach," "sunset," "ocean," "waves," and "sunset over ocean." 

**Don't: Use Irrelevant or Repetitive Keywords**

Avoid using keywords that don’t directly relate to your footage. For instance, using "mountain" for a beach scene is misleading and can frustrate buyers. Also, avoid repeating the same keyword multiple times; search algorithms are smart enough to recognize this as spammy behavior.

## Categories: Place Your Footage Correctly

**Do: Choose the Most Accurate Categories**

Categories help buyers narrow down their search. If you’re uploading a clip of a bustling city street, choose categories like "Urban" or "City Life." This places your footage in the right context for buyers who are specifically browsing those sections.

**Don't: Misclassify Your Footage**

Misclassifying footage can lead to lower visibility and sales. Placing a city scene under "Nature" is not only inaccurate but also misleading for buyers. Double-check the category guidelines of each platform to ensure proper classification.

## Technical Details: Quality Assurance

**Do: Ensure Technical Accuracy**

Before uploading, check all technical details such as resolution, frame rate, and format. For instance, if your footage is shot in 4K, ensure that it’s labeled correctly. Buyers often filter searches by resolution, and incorrect information can lead to missed opportunities.

**Don't: Ignore Platform Specifications**

Each platform has its own set of technical requirements and specifications. Failing to adhere to these can result in your footage being rejected or not displaying correctly. Always review the specifications for platforms like Adobe Stock and Shutterstock before uploading.

## Metadata Review Checklist

Here’s a concise checklist to run through before uploading your next batch:

| Component       | Do                                                     | Don't                                                   |
|-----------------|--------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------|
| **Title**       | Use descriptive and specific titles                    | Use vague or redundant titles                           |
| **Description** | Provide contextual details                             | Overload with keywords                                  |
| **Keywords**    | Use relevant and varied keywords                       | Use irrelevant or repetitive keywords                   |
| **Categories**  | Choose the most accurate categories                    | Misclassify your footage                                |
| **Technical**   | Ensure technical accuracy and platform compliance      | Ignore platform specifications                          |

## FAQ

### What’s the best way to generate keywords for my footage?

Start by brainstorming a list of terms that are directly related to your footage. Think about the setting, action, objects, and emotions conveyed in the clip. Tools like ClipMeta’s [Metadata Grader](https://clipmeta.com/tools/metadata-grader) can help refine your keyword list by suggesting additional terms and ensuring you haven’t missed any critical ones.

### How often should I review and update my metadata?

Metadata isn’t a set-it-and-forget-it task. Regularly review your metadata, especially for clips that aren’t performing as expected. Trends and search behavior can change over time, and updating your metadata can help improve visibility and sales.

### Can metadata affect the ranking of my footage on stock platforms?

Absolutely. Metadata plays a significant role in how footage is ranked and discovered on stock platforms. Accurate and well-structured metadata can improve your footage’s search ranking, making it more likely to be seen by potential buyers.
